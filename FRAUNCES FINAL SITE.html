1. Historical Inspiration: Fraunces was made by Phaedra Charles and Flavia Zimbardi, and inspired by the work of 18th-century French typographer and punchcutter Pierre Simon Fournier. Fournier was known for his elegant and legible typefaces, which reflected the Enlightenment ideals of the time, emphasizing clarity and rationality in design. The digital revival of Fournier's typefaces, such as "Fraunces," serves to maintain a connection to the aesthetic and philosophical underpinnings of the Enlightenment in contemporary typography.
 
2. Design Characteristics: Fraunces is a transitional serif typeface, meaning it bridges the gap between the old-style serifs of the 17th and 18th centuries and the more modern, or didone, serifs that emerged in the 19th century. The font features a high contrast between thick and thin strokes, bracketed serifs, and a vertical stress, which are hallmarks of transitional typefaces. These design elements give Fraunces a refined and somewhat formal appearance, making it suitable for a variety of applications where a sense of tradition and elegance is desired.
 
3. Adaptation for Digital Usage: The original design of Pierre Simon Fournier's typefaces was optimized for print. However, when creating the digital version of Fraunces, Richard Lipton made careful adjustments to ensure that the font would be legible and functional across various digital platforms. This includes improving its performance at smaller sizes on-screen, which can be particularly challenging for serif typefaces with intricate details. The digitization process involved analyzing the original typeface and applying principles of human visual perception to enhance its readability in digital environments, thus adapting an historical design for modern use.
